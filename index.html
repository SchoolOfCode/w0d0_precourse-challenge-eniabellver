Margaret Hamilton

Software Engineer

About

Margaret Heafield Hamilton is an American computer scientist and one of the first computer programmers, she is amongst the people credited for inventing the term "software engineer". Margaret is known for leading the software team that developed the guidance and navigation system for NASA's Apollo spacecraft, which was critical for landing astronauts on the Moon in 1969.

Timeline

1936

Early Life

She is born on August 17 in Paoli, Indiana, US. Her family later moves to Michigan, US.

1954

High School

Graduates from Hancock High School, Michigan, US.

1955

University

Starts studying mathematics at the University of Michigan and later transfers to Earlham College.

1958

Degree

Earns a B.A. in mathematics with a minor in Philosophy and on June 15 marries James Cox Hamilton, a major in chemistry who she met while at Earlham College.

1959

Norton Lorenz Lab

She begins working for Edward Norton Lorenz in the meteorology department at MIT, where she develops software for predicting weather. Her work contributed to Lorenz's publications on chaos theory. Her daughter Lauren is born on November 10.

1961

The S.A.G.E. Project

Until 1963, she works on the Semi-Automatic Ground Environment (SAGE) project at the MIT Lincoln Lab, which is soon developed for military use in anti-aircraft defense. In a conference she said:

What they used to do when you came into this organization as a beginner, was to assign you this program which nobody was able to ever figure out or get to run. When I was the beginner they gave it to me as well. And what had happened was it was tricky programming, and the person who wrote it took delight in the fact that all of his comments were in Greek and Latin. So I was assigned this program and I actually got it to work. It even printed out its answers in Latin and Greek. I was the first one to get it to work.

1963

The Apollo Program

Joins the Charles Stark Draper Laboratory at MIT, which worked on the Apollo Space Mission. Initially hired as a programmer who moved into systems design, she eventually becomes the leader of the team in charge of developing the in-flight software for the Apollo command module, lunar lander and Skylab.

1969

The Moon Landing

In July, Apollo 11 lands humans on the Moon for the first time. On a critical moment, the Apollo Guidance Computer developed by Hamilton's team averted an abort of the Moon landing. Minutes before landing, several computer alarms went off interrupting the astronauts' normal displays. Hamilton was prepared for this situation and designed the software to prevent the astronauts and computer from slipping out of sync while still notifying them of priority tasks.

1976

Higher Order Software

A year after leaving MIT, she co-founds Higher Order Software (HOS) with Saydean Zeldin to develop ideas about error prevention and fault tolerance based on her experience working at MIT creating the Apollo program. She is the CEO of HOS until 1984 and leaves the company in 1985.

1986

Hamilton Technologies

Founds Hamilton Technologies, a company developed around the Universal Systems Language (USL) and the associated automated environment, the 001 Tool Suite, which is based on the preventive concept of "Developing Before the Fact". On this year she also receives the Ada Lovelace Award by the Association for Women in Computing.

2003

NASA Award

Receives the NASA Exceptional Space Act Award for scientific and technical contributions.

2006

Earlham Award

Receives the Outstanding Alumni Award by Earlham College.

2016

Medal of Freedom

Is given the Presidential Medal of Freedom, the highest civilian honor in the US, during Barack Obama's presidency.

2017

Computer History Museum Award

Receives the Computer History Museum Fellow Award, which honors exceptional people whose computing ideas have changed the world.

2018

What the Errors Tell Us

Publishes "What the Errors Tell Us" on IEEE Software magazine, where she talks about her experiences over the last 60 years and how a "theory of errors" was derived from the errors made along the way, of which the axioms of control led to developing the Universal Systems Language (USL), its automation and preventative paradigm.

2017

Latest Awards

Is awarded The Washington Award, an honorary doctorate by Bard College and the Intrepid Lifetime Achievement Award.

"We had to find a way and we did. Looking back, we were the luckiest people in the world; there was no choice but to be pioneers."
